Best VPNs for Windows 11 in 2026 (Privacy & Speed Tested)

Your ISP sees every website you visit. These are the VPNs that actually protect your privacy without making your Windows 11 PC unusably slow.

July 12, 2026 Melalew Mengistu

Last year, a major ISP in the US was caught selling anonymized browsing data to advertisers. "Anonymized" turned out to mean they removed your name but kept everything else the sites you visited, how long you stayed, the search terms you used, and rough location data that could identify your household. The data was detailed enough that researchers re-identified specific individuals within the dataset.

A VPN prevents this by encrypting your traffic before it leaves your computer. Your ISP sees an encrypted connection to a server somewhere, nothing more. But not all VPNs are equal some are slow, some keep logs, and some can't even unblock Netflix. We tested the major options on Windows 11 to find which ones actually deliver on their promises.

What a VPN Actually Does on Windows 11

When you connect to the internet normally, your traffic goes from your PC to your router, through your ISP's network, and out to the destination server. Your ISP can see every domain you connect to, and in the case of unencrypted HTTP sites, the actual content.

A VPN creates an encrypted tunnel from your PC to a server operated by the VPN provider. All your traffic routes through this tunnel. Your ISP sees only an encrypted connection to a single IP address. The websites you visit see the VPN server's IP address instead of yours. Two things change: who can see your traffic (only the VPN provider) and where it appears to come from (the VPN server's location).

On Windows 11 specifically, VPN support matters because the OS sends telemetry data, has background processes that phone home, and integrates with cloud services that expose your IP. A system-level VPN app catches all of this, not just browser traffic.

Who Actually Needs a VPN
  • Anyone who regularly connects to public Wi-Fi at cafes, airports, or hotels.
  • Remote workers whose company VPN is slow and want a personal alternative.
  • People who want to access streaming content restricted to other countries.
  • Anyone who prefers their ISP not build a browsing profile on them.
How We Tested

We installed each VPN's Windows 11 app on the same machine and ran tests over three different connections: a 250 Mbps home connection, a 100 Mbps office connection, and a 30 Mbps mobile hotspot.

1 Speed retention: percentage of baseline speed kept with VPN active.
2 Streaming: whether it unblocks Netflix US, BBC iPlayer, and Disney+ from outside those regions.
3 Privacy: independent audit status, jurisdiction, and data retention policy.
4 Windows 11 integration: kill switch reliability, split tunneling, and startup behavior.

Why It Stands Out

  • NordLynx protocol (their WireGuard implementation) consistently kept 90%+ of our baseline speed the best result we saw.
  • Unblocked Netflix US, UK, Japan, and BBC iPlayer on every test run without needing to switch servers.
  • Threat Protection feature blocks ads, trackers, and malware at the network level works system-wide on Windows 11, not just in the browser.

  • More expensive than Surfshark, especially on the 1-year plan. The 2-year plan is where the pricing becomes reasonable.
Who should buy it: Anyone who wants a VPN that works reliably without tinkering. If you connect to a server and expect it to work for streaming, for privacy, for speed NordVPN delivers that consistently. Worth the premium if you use a VPN daily.

Why It Stands Out

  • Unlimited devices means you can put it on every phone, tablet, laptop, and PC in your household no counting connections.

  • Clean Windows app without the promotional notifications that NordVPN pushes. Lighter on system resources.

Trade-offs

  • Fewer servers in less popular locations. If you need a VPN for a specific country in Africa or Central Asia, Surfshark may not have one.
  • Netherlands jurisdiction is fine for privacy, but it's a Nine Eyes alliance member slightly less privacy-friendly than Panama.
Who should buy it: Families or anyone with more than 5 devices who don't want to pay per-device pricing. Also the right pick if you primarily need a VPN for privacy and casual use, not for reliable streaming unblocking.

Why It Stands Out

  • The free tier is genuinely unlimited no data caps, no bandwidth limits, no speed throttling. Most free VPNs limit you to 500 MB per day or throttle you to 2 Mbps.

  • Speed retention is the lowest of our three picks. On our 250 Mbps connection, we got about 185 Mbps still fast, but noticeably below NordVPN's 225 Mbps.
  • Streaming unblocking doesn't work on the free tier. You need the paid version, and even then it's less reliable than NordVPN.
Who should buy it: Start with the free version if you're not sure you need a VPN. It's good enough for daily privacy on public Wi-Fi. Upgrade to paid only if you need specific server locations or reliable streaming unblocking.

About free VPNs not listed here: Avoid any free VPN from the app store that isn't Proton. There is no such thing as a free VPN that's both privacy-respecting and profitable without a paid tier to subsidize it. Proton funds its free tier through paid ProtonMail and Proton VPN Plus subscriptions.

Buying Guide: What Actually Matters

Protocol: WireGuard or nothing

In 2026, there's no reason to use OpenVPN or IKEv2 for daily use. WireGuard (and its variants like NordLynx) provides equivalent encryption with significantly less overhead about 4,000 lines of code compared to OpenVPN's 100,000. This translates to faster connections, quicker reconnections when you switch Wi-Fi networks, and better battery life on laptops.

All three of our picks support WireGuard. If a VPN you're considering doesn't, it's behind.

Kill switch: Non-negotiable

A kill switch blocks all internet traffic if the VPN connection drops. Without it, your real IP address leaks out the moment the VPN disconnects which happens more often than you'd think, especially on Windows 11 when the OS manages network transitions. Look for an "always-on" kill switch option in the Windows app settings.

Split tunneling: Useful once you set it up

Split tunneling lets you route some apps through the VPN and others through your regular connection. Common use case: run your browser through the VPN but let games and video calls use your direct connection to avoid the latency increase. All three picks support app-level split tunneling on Windows 11.

No-logs policy vs no-logs reality

Every VPN claims a "no-logs" policy. The difference is whether they've been independently audited to verify it. NordVPN has the strongest track record here in 2018, Turkish authorities seized one of their servers and found no logs. They've since commissioned three more audits that confirmed the same result. Surfshark and Proton have also passed independent audits, but with fewer real-world tests.

Don't confuse "no-logs" with "anonymous." Even with a strict no-logs VPN, you still create an account with your email address and pay with a credit card or PayPal. If absolute anonymity matters to you, pay with cryptocurrency and use a separate email address. Proton VPN is the only one of our picks that lets you sign up without an email on their free tier.

Windows 11 specific considerations

  • Check that the VPN app supports Windows 11's ARM architecture if you're on a Snapdragon-powered device. Not all VPNs have ARM-compatible apps yet.
  • Disable Windows' own VPN if you've previously configured one through Settings → Network & Internet → VPN having two active VPN profiles can cause routing conflicts.
  • Set the VPN app to launch at startup if you want always-on protection. But be aware this adds 3–5 seconds to your boot time on most machines.
  • If you use Windows' built-in DNS over HTTPS, the VPN's DNS feature may conflict with it. Let the VPN handle DNS when it's active it's more private that way.

Frequently Asked Questions

Using a VPN is legal in most countries including the US, UK, Canada, and most of Europe. A few countries restrict or ban VPNs notably China, Russia, Iran, and UAE. Using a VPN to commit illegal acts like downloading copyrighted material remains illegal regardless of whether a VPN is active.

All VPNs introduce some speed loss because your data travels through an extra server and gets encrypted. With WireGuard-based VPNs on a modern PC, expect 7–15% speed loss on a 250 Mbps connection. You'll still get 210+ Mbps more than enough for 4K streaming. The loss is more noticeable on very fast connections (500+ Mbps) because the VPN server becomes the bottleneck.

Windows 11 includes a VPN client that can connect to existing VPN servers, but it doesn't provide servers. You still need a VPN service. The built-in client supports IKEv2, L2TP, PPTP, and SSTP all older protocols that are slower and less secure than WireGuard. Most people are better off using the VPN's own Windows app.

Your ISP can see that you're connecting to a known VPN server IP, but cannot see what you're doing inside the encrypted tunnel. If you want to hide even the fact that you're using a VPN, NordVPN and Surfshark offer obfuscated servers that disguise VPN traffic as regular HTTPS useful on networks that actively block VPN connections.

It depends on what you're optimizing for. Always-on provides constant privacy and prevents ISP tracking. But it adds latency that matters for competitive gaming or real-time video calls. Use split tunneling to route only sensitive apps through the VPN browsers and email, for example while letting games and Zoom use your direct connection.
